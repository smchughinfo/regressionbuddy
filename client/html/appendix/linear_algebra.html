Linear Equations in n Variables Two Variables \(Ax+By=C\)
The line crosses the x axis at \(\frac{C}{A}\) when \(A \neq 0\). The line crosses the y axis at \(\frac{C}{B}\) when \(B \neq 0\). The slope of the line is \(-\frac{A}{B}\). Three Variables \(Ax+By+Cz=D\)
The vector \((A,B,C)\) is normal (orthogonal, at a 90 degree angle) to the plane. The distance from the origin to point on the plane closest to the origin is \( \frac{\left|D\right|}{\sqrt{A^2+B^2+C^2}} \). n Variables The standard form of a linear equation is \( a_1^\hphantom{1} x_1^\hphantom{1} + a_2^\hphantom{1} x_2^\hphantom{1} +a_3^\hphantom{1} x_3^\hphantom{1} + \cdot \; \cdot \; \cdot \; + a_n^\hphantom{1} x_n^\hphantom{1} = b \). The coeffecients \(a_1^\phantom{1} \), \(a_2^\phantom{1} \), \(a_3^\phantom{1} \), . . . , \(a_n^\phantom{1}\) and the constant term \(b\) are real numbers. The Number of Solutions to a System of Linear Equations A solution of a system linear equations is a point that every equation in the system touches.

A system of linear equations can have no solutions, one solution, or an infinite number of solutions.

  1. Systems with no solutions are called inconsistent.
  2. Systems that have a solution are called consistent.

If \(x_4^\phantom{1}=1\) and \(x_3^\phantom{1}=2\) there is no way \(x_3^\phantom{1}+x_4^\phantom{1}\) can equal \(4\), so the system is inconsistent.

One Solution \begin{align*} x_1^\phantom{1} + x_2^\phantom{1} + x_3^\phantom{1} + x_4^\phantom{1} + x_5^\phantom{1} & = 1 \\ \phantom{x_1^1+}x_2^\phantom{1} + x_3^\phantom{1} + x_4^\phantom{1} + x_5^\phantom{1} &= 2 \\ \phantom{x_1^1+x_2^1}x_3^\phantom{1} + x_4^\phantom{1} + x_5^\phantom{1} &= 8 \\ \phantom{x_1^1-x_2^1+x_3^1}x_4^\phantom{1} + x_5^\phantom{1} &= 4 \\ \phantom{x_1^1-x_2^1+x_3^1+x_4^1}x_5^\phantom{1} &= 2 \\ \end{align*}

If \(x_5^\phantom{1}=2\) then \(x_4^\phantom{1}=2\), \(x_3^\phantom{1}=4\), \(x_2^\phantom{1}=-6\), and \(x_1^\phantom{1}=-1\) so the system has only one solution.

Infinite Solutions \begin{align*} x_1^\phantom{1} + x_2^\phantom{1} + x_3^\phantom{1} + x_4^\phantom{1} + x_5^\phantom{1} & = 1 \\ \phantom{x_1^1+}x_2^\phantom{1} + x_3^\phantom{1} + x_4^\phantom{1} + x_5^\phantom{1} &= 2 \\ \phantom{x_1^1+x_2^1}x_3^\phantom{1} + x_4^\phantom{1} + x_5^\phantom{1} &= 8 \\ \phantom{x_1^1-x_2^1+x_3^1}x_4^\phantom{1} + x_5^\phantom{1} &= 4 \\ \phantom{x_1^1-x_2^1+x_3^1+x_4^1}x_5^\phantom{1} &= t \end{align*}

In this case \(x_5^\phantom{1}\) equals the variable \(t\) . Since the values of the other variables change as \(x_5^\phantom{1}\) changes this system has an infinite number of solutions.

Back-Substitution Once you've found a variable in one equation you can use it in the other equations. Back-Substitution Example We are given \(z=7\). \begin{align*} x+y+z &= 5 \\ \phantom{x+}y+z &= 4 \\ \phantom{x+y+}z &= 7 \Leftarrow \end{align*}
Substitute \(7\) for \(z\) and solve to get \(y=-3\). \begin{align*} x+y+ z &= 5 \\ \phantom{x+}y+ 7 &= 4 \Leftarrow \\ \phantom{x+y+}z &= 7 \end{align*}
Substitute \(7\) for \(z\) and \(-3\) for \(y\) and solve to get \(x=1\). \begin{align*} x -3 + 7 &= \;5 \Leftarrow \\ \phantom{x-}y\phantom{+7}\;\, &= \text{-}3 \\ \phantom{x+y+}z &= \;7 \end{align*}
Result: \begin{align*} x &= \phantom{-}1 \\ y &= -3 \\ z &= \phantom{-}7 \end{align*}
