One-Sided and Two-Sided Limits One-Sided Limits
  1. Left-sided limits are evaluated as \( x \rightarrow a \) from the left.
    \( \lim\limits_{x\to\ a^{-}} f(x)=L \)
  2. Right-sided limits are evaluated as \( x \rightarrow a \) from the right.
    \( \lim\limits_{x\to\ a^{+}} f(x)=L \)
Two-sided Limits Two-sided limits are just regular limits. For a two-sided limit to exist both the left-sided and right-sided limits must exist and be equal. Example 1 /images/calculus-appendix/one-sided-limit-example-1.svg https://www.geogebra.org/graphing/QgyQv3JX \( f(x) = \) \begin{cases} x^{2}, & \text{if x < 1} \\ ln(x), & \text{if x $\geq$ 1} \end{cases}
\( \lim\limits_{x\to\ 1^{-}} f(x)= 1 \)
\( \lim\limits_{x\to\ 1^{+}} f(x) = 0 \)
Since \( \lim\limits_{x\to\ 1^{-}} f(x) \neq \lim\limits_{x\to\ 1^{+}} f(x) \) the two-sided limit does not exist.
Example 2 /images/calculus-appendix/one-sided-limit-example-2.svg https://www.geogebra.org/graphing/yFzXGzY2 \( f(x) = \) \begin{cases} cos(x), & \text{if x $\neq -\frac{\pi}{2}$} \\ \frac{3}{2}, & \text{if x = -$\frac{\pi}{2}$} \end{cases}
\( \lim\limits_{x\to\ -\frac{\pi}{2}^{-}} f(x) = 0 \)
\( \lim\limits_{x\to\ -\frac{\pi}{2}^{+}} f(x) = 0 \)
\( \lim\limits_{x\to\ -\frac{\pi}{2}} f(x) = \lim\limits_{x\to\ -\frac{\pi}{2}^{-}} f(x) = \lim\limits_{x\to\ -\frac{\pi}{2}^{+}} f(x) = 0 \)
Example 3 /images/calculus-appendix/one-sided-limit-example-3.svg https://www.geogebra.org/graphing/yFzXGzY2 \( f(x) = \frac{1}{x} \)
\( \lim\limits_{x\to\ 0^{-}} f(x) \) does not exit.
\( \lim\limits_{x\to\ 0^{+}} f(x) \) does not exit.
Since one side of this limit, and in fact both sides, do not exist the two-sided limit does not exist.
