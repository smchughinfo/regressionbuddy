Continuity A function \(f(x)\) is continuous at \(x=a\) if: \( \lim\limits_{x\to\ a} f(x) = f(a) \) Continuous Function /images/calculus-appendix/sinx.svg https://www.geogebra.org/graphing/b6s7Edt9 \( f(x)=sin(x)\)

This graph has no breaks. It can be drawn without lifting your pencil from the paper.

Jump Discontinuity /images/calculus-appendix/discontinuity-example-1.svg https://www.geogebra.org/graphing/NxjJQM5R \( f(x) = \) \begin{cases} x, & \text{if x $\leq$ 1} \\ x+1, & \text{if x > 1} \end{cases}

This graph approaches a different value for \(x=1\) if you come from the left vs. if you come from the right.

Removable Discontinuity /images/calculus-appendix/discontinuity-example-2.svg https://www.geogebra.org/graphing/JFMCT5Cs \( f(x) = \) \begin{cases} -1, & \text{if x < -1} \\ x, & \text{if x < 2} \\ 3, & \text{if = 2} \\ 2, & \text{if > 2} \\ \end{cases}

This graph approaches \(2\) as you come from the left and right but the value at \(x=2\) is not 2.

Essential Discontinuity /images/calculus-appendix/discontinuity-example-3.svg https://www.geogebra.org/graphing/hG3XCMg6 \( f(x)=\frac{1}{x}\)

In this graph not only is there a different value as you approach \(x=0\) from the left and right but the values themselves are \(-\infty\) and \(\infty\), respectively. Both are conditions for an essential discontinuity.
