Jump Discontinuity /images/calculus-appendix/discontinuity-example-1.svg https://www.geogebra.org/graphing/NxjJQM5R \( f(x) = \) \begin{cases} x, & \text{if x $\leq$ 1} \\ x+1, & \text{if x > 1} \end{cases}

This graph approaches a different value for \(x=1\) if you come from the left vs. if you come from the right.

Removable Discontinuity /images/calculus-appendix/discontinuity-example-2.svg https://www.geogebra.org/graphing/gC4zDVxv \( f(x) = \) \begin{cases} x^{\frac{1}{3}}, & \text{if x $\neq$ 1} \\ 2, & \text{if x = 1} \\ \end{cases}

This graph approaches \(1\) as you come from the left and right but the value at \(x=1\) is \(2\), not \(1\).

Essential Discontinuity /images/calculus-appendix/discontinuity-example-3.svg https://www.geogebra.org/graphing/hG3XCMg6 \( f(x)=\frac{1}{x}\)

In this graph not only is there a different value as you approach \(x=0\) from the left and right but the values themselves are \(-\infty\) and \(\infty\), respectively. Both are conditions for an essential discontinuity. Something something oscilating.
